Peter Gabriel

Religion

Gabriel exists somewhere between Buddhism and Christianity, though he doesn't seem too religious.

Political Views

Gabriel is a liberal and fair-weather supporter of the Labour Party. His real passion lies in international human rights.

Peter Gabriel was born and raised in Surrey, England.

Gabriel was raised in the Christian tradition.1 However, one gets the sense that he’s anything but devout–though Christianity still gurgles in his subconscious. He said:

When I’m thinking of God I’m a Buddhist, when I’m in pain and despair I’m a Christian.2

Clearly, Gabriel is interested in Buddhism–and it appears that it appeals to his higher reasoning, but he has also reasoned that religion is a double-edged sword, something that can be either positive or negative. He compared it to music in this way:

There has always been a strong relationship between music and religion. It is because they both plug directly into the heart and can have real power for good or evil.3

Back to Christianity, Gabriel was the subject of controversy for having written the score for Martin Scorsese’s film, The Last Temptation of Christ. The film drew criticism for not portraying Jesus as a god. But Gabriel wasn’t about to apologize, saying:

If people’s faith is so weak that it can be destroyed by a film, then it really isn’t much to begin with. I think people may find themselves reviewing their own lives and their own points of view on religion as a result of the film. I’m very proud to have been a part of it.4

Labour and liberalism

Gabriel is highly political. In England, he’s made the biggest mark as a supporter of the Labour Party. In 1997, he was listed as one of the party’s most generous contributors5 and was a supporter of Tony Blair until he sided with George W. Bush and the Americans on the Iraq War. He said:

This is a fundamental issue of life and death and I very much think the Prime Minister is in the wrong. I’m also sure George Bush is an affable bloke but he’s highly dangerous and I wish America was in the hands of someone else.6

When it comes to social issues, Gabriel is quite liberal. He’s vocally in favor of choice regarding abortion.7 He allowed a candidate of Britain’s Green Party to use one of his songs,8 which might not necessarily mean anything except that he got a bit upset when American super-conservative, Rush Limbaugh, used one of his songs on his radio show. Gabriel demanded he stop.9

Non-controversial, but no less important, is Gabriel’s involvement with international organizations like Amnesty International, for which he went on two world tours, among other things, and was ultimately given the organization’s highest honor.10
