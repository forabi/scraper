Tulisa Contostavlos

Religion

Contostavlos spent much of her life wild and secular, but now she has a strong Christian, maybe Catholic, faith.

Political Views

She is focused on what the government can do to help teens in tough situations, and she's sounds like a Labour-supporter.

Tula “Tulisa” Contostavlos was born and raised in London, England.

Contostavlos had a tough, unstable upbringing–which we’ll get into later–so it wasn’t much of an environment for consistent religious practice. She did go to a Catholic school for a time growing up, but she says she wasn’t religious.1

That all changed, though, after a little sex tape incident. She said her wild days are behind her and she’s found a new path:

I’m a lot more religious now. I kind of stopped believing in God but as I got older I found my faith again — and with that has come morals.2

A friend of hers confirmed that she prays quite often.3 But that doesn’t mean she’s all over Twitter talking about God or prayers or being blessed. She doesn’t even appear to wear a cross, and I found no sighting of her at church. But we’ll take her word for it anyway. And even though she hasn’t explicitly said so, we’ll assume she went back towards Catholicism.

The Politics of a North London Girl

Contostavlos has a lot of opinions about what the government can do to help young people, and it all comes from her experiences growing up. From the age of 11, after her father left, the young pop star was the sole caregiver of her mother, who was later diagnosed with schizoaffective disorder. Her mother’s erratic behavior–including hallucinations, paranoia, and huge mood swings–and her frequent hospital stays, were so hard to cope with that Contostavlos tried to commit suicide twice in her teens. She says there needs to be more help from the government for young people like herself:

Mental healthcare in this country is much better now, although we still have a long way to go. Too often people like me are just left to get on with it.4

Likewise, she thinks there needs to be more support for young people in tough neighborhoods. Schools, she says, should be better at deterring kids from carrying knives. And the government needs to fund safe places for kids to go after school:

How can you not be sorting things out when kids are dying out on the streets? . . . Look, if they won’t give kids youth clubs or places to go during afternoons and evenings, then how can the Government expect to cut down on yobs hanging on the street and crime rates? You need to give young people places to go–and fund them. And if they want knife crime to go down then we need proper safety checks.5

She also said she wants to keep University fees low, and lower the price of public transportation.6 Sounds like a good fit for Labour (or possibly the Socialist Party), don’t you think? I couldn’t find any specific incidents where she endorsed the party or any of its candidates, but I think it’s a pretty good bet she’s not voting for David Cameron. Considering Contostavlos’ candor in interviews, I’d say we’ll hear more from this singer as the years pass. Keep us updated in the comments.
