The religions and political views of the influentials.

M.I.A.

Religion

She may be Hindu, but hasn't explicitly said so.

Political Views

As a Sri Lankan Tamil refugee, she calls the actions of the government there a genocide.

M.I.A., whose real name is Mathangi “Maya” Arulpragasam, was born in London, England. She grew up mostly in Jaffna, Sri Lanka until her family moved back to London during the Sri Lankan civil war.

I couldn’t find any direct quotes from M.I.A. about her current religious beliefs, but all signs point to Hindu. Her family are Sri Lankan Tamils, a predominantly Hindu population. And those who speculate or claim to know about her current religious beliefs mostly say she practices Hinduism. One site said she “has practiced all her life,”1 and another said she practices Batticaloa Hinduism.2

One site claimed she was an atheist Muslim,3 which might be because she wore the niqab, a Muslim garment worn by women that covers the whole head and face except the eyes, to a red-carpet event.4 She didn’t comment on her fashion choice for the evening, so we’re left to wonder exactly what statement she was trying to make. But considering that France passed a law to ban the wearing of the niqab in public just a few months earlier,5 it’s not a stretch to think she was making a statement against such laws.

But in the end, M.I.A. remains a bit of a mystery. Maybe she feels like there are more pressing matters than talking about how she worships. Like, say, war and politics.

Refugees, Terrorists, and the Pop Star

M.I.A.’s childhood absolutely defines her aggressive and unapologetic political views. Her father, whom she barely knew, was a member of a Sri Lankan militant guerrilla group, the Tamil Tigers, which meant her family had to live in hiding to avoid harassment from the army. After living in poverty and violence throughout her first decade, she moved with her siblings and mother to England to escape civil war–and then she grew up as a poor refugee in a country not known for its tolerance of immigration.6

She now outspokenly calls the actions of the Sri Lankan government a genocide against the Tamil people. And her sympathetic view of the Tamil Tigers have caused some to call her a terrorist sympathizer–an accusation she vehemently denied.7 On the topic of terrorism, particularly the United States’ War on Terror, she said,

You can’t separate the world into two parts like that, good and evil. Terrorism is a method, but America has successfully tied all these pockets of independence struggles, revolutions, and extremists into one big notion of terrorism.8

Her political opinions–or more accurately, statements–about any topic are not easily confined to a label, and are too numerous and complex to do them any justice here. They don’t make her sound left or right, but show how deeply she cares about those in the world who live in violence. She seems to want to emphasize less how she feels about different issues, so much as to provoke thought in those who listen. She said,

There’s so much confusion about what I stand for and what I’m saying that that’s the whole point: there have to be discussions; there has to be people talking, and there has to be young people talking about politics if they want. They have to have a chance to hear different opinions. And that’s really what it’s about.9

If you’re interesting in seeing exactly how far M.I.A. is willing to push the limits of what’s acceptable in mainstream pop culture, watch this video from her song, “Born Free,” depicting an imaginary military genocide against red-heads in the U.S. And yeah, it’s pretty horrifying, so don’t watch it in front of the kids.
